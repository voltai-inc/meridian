# DC Workstream Update — Week of July 7–11, 2026

**TL;DR:** the field told us where the pain is (the utility process gates everything, and even
"powered" sites come with conceptual, fast-decaying answers), we shipped the full pipeline as a
live demo (find → evaluate → review → procure), and the strategy is written down in one place
(`PRODUCT_PICTURE.md`). This update collects the evidence for *why this path*, what shipped,
and the directions on the table.

## 1 · What the field taught us — why we went this way

Three artifacts from this week's utility work, and what each one proves:

### The Entergy calls (LA — Jul 6 · MS — Jul 8)

- A **Class 5 transmission study is $50K, non-refundable, per site, per load scenario**, takes
  2–3 months — and is **valid for only ~60 days**. Site answers decay faster than deals move.
- Entergy **can't assess sites in bulk** — you must narrow to your top ~3 before submitting.
  So the screening decision (*which* sites, at *what MW*) happens before the utility will even
  engage — with real money on each guess.
- **Mississippi mandates interruptible service** for all data centers — Entergy can shed the
  load at any time, unpredictably. Backup/BESS sizing against the real workload shape stops
  being an optimization and becomes the design question.
- **15–20 MW+ means a customer-owned substation: 36–48 months.** The service agreement runs
  10–15 years with a minimum-bill clause and collateral. Small siting errors compound into
  decade-scale commitments.

### The Project Atria RFI (Gallman, MS — 10 MW request)

Entergy's own RFI-level answer for a *specific powered-adjacent site*: rebuild 0.52 mi of
distribution, capacitor banks, ~$950K, 7–12 months — and, in their words:

> "No detailed studies or power flow analysis was performed in the development of this RFI
> level assessment. All information presented should be thought of as conceptual and subject
> to change… This estimate does not include long lead times on materials."

**"Powered" is a claim, not a fact** — even the utility's first answer is explicitly
provisional. This is the confidence-ladder argument in the utility's own words.

### The intake form (Entergy's data center intake, Jul 8)

What they require before advancing: 15-year ESA commitment, upgrade costs on the customer,
3-month deposit, the $50K study fee, liquid collateral — and then the technical asks:
**requested MW, ramp schedule by year, backup generation type, site plan/KMZ, load profile
questionnaire** (power factor called out as critical on the LA call).

We stared at that form without confident answers. That's the product insight: **half the
technical fields are exactly what Meridian computes** — usable MW by design, ramp behavior,
backup strategy under interruptible service, the load profile of a synchronized AI fleet.
Nobody on either side of the table can characterize an AI training load today; we can.

### The conclusion this evidence forces

The utility process — not land, not capital, not equipment — is the binding constraint for
every site in the pipeline. So the leverage points are exactly the three places the toolkit
plays: **before the study** (which sites are worth $50K, at what MW), **into the study**
(the load characterization the questionnaires demand), and **after interruptible terms**
(backup/BESS sizing against the real workload shape). Full argument: `PRODUCT_PICTURE.md`.

## 3 · Strategic directions on the table

1. **The reviewer posture, confirmed.** We author requirements and review designs — never the
   one-line itself. Same shape as schematic review, one level up. Shipped as the Design Review
   frame; the arcturus lineage (knowledge → review → generation) is the roadmap curve.
2. **Demand-side optimization (from the July 9 EE conversation).** GPU power as a nonlinear
   function of temperature × workload; optimize placement inside a fixed MW envelope. Meridian
   verifies, the optimizer generates. **Gate before investing:** confirm it isn't already
   solved inside NVIDIA/Supermicro (GPU power-curve data via chip team / partners).
3. **The load characterization as the first paid artifact.** The Class 5 questionnaire and the
   intake forms literally request what Meridian outputs. Cheapest wedge into real deals: fill
   our own intake forms from Meridian, then offer the same package to other developers facing
   the same blank form.
4. **The "own it" question.** The team framing this week: Voltai as *pseudo off-taker* —
   validating designs before real off-takers commit — with a find-and-sell site model rather
   than owning/operating. The toolkit serves both postures: it's our internal edge on our own
   site deals first, and the external product for developers/EPCs second. The first "customer"
   is us, on the sites in the tracker; a design partner converts it into a product decision.

**Where we play — one line for the deck:** across the whole pipeline. Find the site, verify
what it carries, characterize the load for the utility, review the design, hand procurement a
BOM — verified intelligence at every step, authorship of none of the physical design. The
partner-facing version of this story is in the
[Voltai × Hyundai Motors deck](https://claude.ai/design/p/8f12ac34-6fb4-4157-a63d-5ffedce25ac3?file=Votla+Hyundai+Motors.dc.html&via=share).
