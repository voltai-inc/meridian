Voltai's evaluation platform models an AI data center from the workload down: the developer inputs grid capacity, chip choice, and cooling configuration — the platform simulates what the actual training or inference fleet will draw, stress-tests the electrical design against it, and produces the spec sheet an off-taker or financier needs. Screening that today takes an engineering engagement happens in minutes, pre-build.

The gap it closes

Today the industry simulates data center load at a flat utilization percentage — one number for every workload. That misses what actually stresses a site:

  • Per-model power draw. A dense frontier model and a sparse mixture-of-experts model on the same fleet swing the grid at different frequencies, with different checkpoint signatures and peaks.
  • Adjacent racks. Concurrent tenants — two training jobs and an inference fleet — pull visibly different power on the same electrical chain, at every timestep.
  • Transients. A site that passes steady-state analysis can fail on checkpoint cliffs, synchronized ramps, or forced oscillation in the grid's sensitive band.

What it does

  • Workload power simulation — per-model traces derived from architecture (step frequency, checkpoint size/length, MoE vs dense duty), checked against the full electrical chain: 2N/N-1, islanded gensets, ramp and oscillation screens, BESS credit.
  • Envelope sign-off — sweeps every admissible workload timing and returns the worst case with its counterexample: the pre-tape-out sign-off discipline, applied to a facility.
  • Serving & capacity planning — first-principles prefill/decode sizing per model, traffic and SLA; heterogeneous fleets fall out of the physics.
  • Feasibility → collateral — utility-study ingestion, design rationale, cost and performance economics, off-taker spec sheet.
