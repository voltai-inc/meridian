Per-workload power simulation for AI data centers — design specification brief

The industry models data center load as a flat utilization percentage — one number for every workload. This system simulates the actual workload: which model is training or serving, on which fleet, with what timing — and carries that trace through the full electrical chain to a sign-off verdict and off-taker spec sheet. This brief describes the inputs, the models, the checks, and the integration paths, for partner engineering review. Figures derived from public data are marked ESTIMATED; measured telemetry replaces each 1:1.

02 · Workload → power model (the core)

Per-model trace derivation. The training trace is derived from the architecture, not assumed ESTIMATED:

  • Step period — 6 FLOP/param/token over the fleet's effective throughput under a weak-scaling batch recipe: the model sets the grid-forcing frequency. Across five frontier open-weight models on one 200 MW site, forcing spans 0.03–1.7 Hz — some inside the grid's sensitive oscillation band, some out.
  • Checkpoint signature — optimizer state (≈14 B/param, mixed-precision Adam) over the storage path sets dip depth and length: 1–9 TB, 5–19 s for the same five models.
  • Architecture duty — dense (collectives overlapped, long high-power phase, full peak) vs. sparse MoE (all-to-all exposed, shorter duty, softer peak, busier low phase).
  • Chip shaping — on-board energy storage damps the synchronized swing per SKU; cooling type sets the mechanical power ratio and lag.

Fleet composition — the adjacent-racks question.

  • Concurrent tenants (e.g. two training jobs with de-correlated checkpoint phase + an inference fleet) are simulated per-job and summed — the meter and every shared component see the aggregate; each rack group's draw stays resolved.
  • Inference is modeled as its own regime — smooth diurnal demand plus autocorrelated batch noise; sizing comes from a first-principles roofline serving model (prefill compute-bound / decode bandwidth-bound, per named model, traffic archetype and SLA — heterogeneous fleets fall out of the physics).
  • Output per timestep: per-rack-group draw, adjacent-group differential, diversity credit vs. sum of per-job peaks.

Measured-trace ingestion. Any synthesized trace can be replaced by measured telemetry (CSV import today) — same checks, same reports, verified provenance label.

03 · Electrical simulation & checks

  • Chain model — 2N reference block scaled to site: utility feed → MV switchgear → transformer → LV switchgear → UPS → PDU/busway, with per-component ratings, short-time factors and efficiencies; loading recorded at each node, normal and N-1 (full load on the surviving path during the worst transient).
  • BESS credit — first-order smoothing (τ = 30 s) against the raw meter trace; power/energy limits enforced; raw exceedance kept as a labeled sensitivity when the BESS is load-bearing.
  • Islanded operation — genset firm capacity and step-load tolerance with BESS bridging, under utility loss + single-path failure.
  • Grid commitments — contracted-capacity headroom, worst 1 s ramp (raw and BESS-smoothed), and a forced-oscillation screen: FFT band-pass 0.05–1 Hz of the meter trace vs. a peak-to-peak limit — periodic training demand can couple to inter-area grid modes.
  • Envelope sign-off — the sign-off question is not "does this timing pass?" but "can any admissible timing break the design?" A bounded-exhaustive sweep (synchronization 25–100%, iteration 1–30 s, checkpoint cadence/length — 96 scenarios) returns the worst case with its exact counterexample: pre-tape-out sign-off discipline applied to a facility.

05 · Integration paths

To the question "is the output a SimReady asset?" — the output is the workload-conditioned load profile and the sign-off evidence around it. That data is designed to plug into three places:

  • Omniverse / SimReady digital twins. We build on the open-source Omniverse layer. Today's digital-twin power/thermal simulations drive from a flat load assumption; our per-rack, per-model load tables can drive them from the actual workload — and per-model power behavior is a natural annotation layer on SimReady assets, so a twin knows that the rack it renders draws differently running a dense frontier model than a sparse MoE.
  • Pre-deployment power planning. For land/power/shell balancing systems, the simulator produces workload-conditioned load forecasts per site — updated as the model mix changes, rather than fixed at a utilization percentage — plus the transient envelope (BESS/genset implications) per candidate deployment.
  • Next-generation silicon. The same derivation runs on unreleased chip parameters: simulated architecture in, facility-level power behavior out — data center readiness evaluated pre-tape-out, alongside the chip.

06 · Validation status & the data ask

Where the model stands. The framework — trace synthesis, chain simulation, checks, envelope sweep — is implemented and self-testing. Calibration is public-spec-derived ESTIMATED: model architectures from open weights, chip power from published specifications, fluctuation characteristics from published operator accounts. No production telemetry has been fitted yet; we label this on every figure rather than blur it.

What measured data replaces, 1:1: amplitude and duty of the iteration swing per SKU, checkpoint signatures per storage architecture, on-board smoothing behavior, cooling-plant transient response, inference fleet noise statistics.

The ask. Rack-level power telemetry for known workloads — even short windows (30–60 min) across a handful of models and SKUs — is sufficient to calibrate and to quantify the estimate error honestly.

In return: every analysis run with partner equipment or data carries a verified — partner data label in front of the developers, off-takers and financiers making the evaluation decision — differentiation at the point where equipment gets specified, before an engineering firm is engaged.

Suggested next step. A working session with the simulation/digital-twin and power-planning teams: we walk through the derivation and checks on a live site, they tell us where it's wrong, and we scope a minimal calibration dataset. We treat correction as the fastest path to a tool the ecosystem can rely on.
